A Walk
with Mrs. Dalloway

A morning in mid June, 1923 — Follow Mrs. Dalloway on her walk to buy flowers, from Westminister to Mayfair

Mrs Dalloway decided she would buy the flowers herself

Begin at Clarissa and Peter's home, on the corner
of Cowley Street and Barton Street
Walk to the end of Barton Street, where you'll find Great College Street
Here, take left and walk to the end of Great College Street
Walk through the small arch on your right hand side
Walk through Dean's Yard, passing the entrance to Westminister Abbey, exiting through the arch on the other side
On the other side of the arch, take a left, and walk up to the traffic lights on the corner
“For having lived in Westminister - how many years now? over twenty, - one feels even in the midst of the traffic, or waking at night, Clarissa was postive, a particular hush, or solemnity; an indescribable pause; suspense (but that might be her heart, affected, they said, by influenza) before Big Ben strikes. There! Out it boomed. First warning, musical; then the hour, irrevocable. The leaden circles dissolved in the air. Such fools we are, she thought, crossing Victoria Street.”
Cross the road and keeping walking straight ahead to Storey's Gate
Walk to the end of Storey's Gate, and here walk across Birdcage Walk and into St James's Park
But how strange, on entering the Park, the silence; the mist; the hum; the slow-swimming happy ducks; the pouched birds waddling; and who should be coming along with his back against the government buildings, most appropriately, carrying a despatch box stamped with the Royal Arms, who but Hugh Whitbread; her old friend - the admirable Hugh!
"Good Morning to you, Clarissa!" said Hugh, rather extravagantly, for they had known each other as children.

"Where are you of to?"
"I love walking in London," said Mrs. Dalloway.

"Really, it's better than walking in the country."
Walk into the middle of the park, finding the lake
Walk around the lake until you reach the Blue Bridge
Then take the path that goes up towards The Mall, finding the park gates
Cross onto the other side of The Mall, and walk up towards Bukingham Place
Keep walking until you reach a path, on your left hand side, that takes you into Green Park
Walk the length of the path, until you reach the top
She had reached the Park gates. She stood for a moment, looking at the omnibuses in Picadilly.
She would say of any one in the world now that they were this or were that. She felt very young; at the same time unspeakably aged. She sliced like a knife through everything; at the same time was outside, looking on. She had a perpetual sense, as she watched the taxi cabs, of being out, out, far out to sea and alone; she always had the feeling that it was very, very dangerous to live even one day.
Take a left outside the gates and keep walking
Her only gift was knowing people almost by instinct, she thought, walking on.
Keep walking on
But everyone remembered; what what she loved was this, here, now in front of her; the fat lady in the cab. Did it matter then, she asked herself, walking towards Bond Street, did it matter that she must inevitably cease completely; all this must go on without her; did she resent it; or did it not become consoling to believe that death ended absolutely?
Keep walking up Piccadilly
Stop at Hatchards, and take a look inside
But what was she dreaming as she looked into Hatchards' shop window? What was she trying to recover?
Cross on to the other side of the road, and start walking back down
How much she wanted it - that people should look pleased as she came in, Clarissa thought and turned and walked back towards Bond Street, annoyed, because it was silly to have other reasons for doing things.
Take Old Bond Street when it comes up on your right hand side
She had the oddest sense of being herself invisible; unseen; unknown; there being no more marrying, no more having of children now, but only this astonishing and rather solemn progress with the rest of them, up Bond Street, this being Mrs. Dalloway; not even Clarissa any more; this being Mrs. Richard Dalloway.
Keep walking up Old Bond Street, carrying on to New Bond Street
Bond Street fascinated her; Bond Street early in the morning in the season; its flags flying; its shops; no splash; no glitter, one roll of tweed in the shop where her father had bought his suits for fifty years; a few pearls; salmon on an iceblock.
Keep walking along New Bond Street
Gloves and shoes; she had a passion for gloves; but her own daughter, her Elizabeth, cared not a straw for either of them. Not a straw, she thought, going on up Bond Street to a shop where they kept flowers for her when she gave a party.
Stop when you reach Brook Street on you left hand side
Clarissa was suspended on one side of Brook Street;
And Clarissa, too, gave a party. She stiffened a little; so she would stand at the top of the stairs.
